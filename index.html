About me

I use economic tools to make sense of how people and institutions respond to rules, incentives, and uncertainty — often in ways that standard assumptions miss.

My work focuses on applied questions at the intersection of policy and institutions, with an emphasis on participation decisions, regulatory thresholds, and equilibrium responses. I combine causal analysis with a strong focus on clarity and decision relevance.

I have experience across academic and applied research environments in Europe and the United States, including Yale University and now at the Ellison Institute of Technology in Oxford.

  • Incentives, enforcement credibility, and participation decisions
  • Institutional and regulatory design in real-world settings
  • Applied empirical analysis for policy and strategy

Featured project

Do symbolic penalties work?

A $0.05 fine that is almost never enforced still coincides with a ~20-point jump in turnout at age 18 — consistent with a large participation response at a legal threshold.

This project uses Argentina’s compulsory voting threshold as a clean test of how participation incentives shift at a regulatory cutoff when expected penalties are near zero. The evidence is most consistent with expressive compliance: the law operates as a normative signal rather than a credible threat.

What is “expressive compliance”?

Behaviour can change not because people fear punishment, but because the law shifts expectations about what is normal or required. In that view, even symbolic penalties can generate meaningful participation responses.
