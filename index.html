I use economic tools to make sense of how people and institutions respond to rules, incentives, and uncertainty — often in ways that standard assumptions miss.

My work focuses on applied questions at the intersection of policy, behaviour, and institutions, combining causal analysis with a strong emphasis on clarity and decision relevance. I have experience across academic and applied research environments in Europe and the United States, including Yale University and the Ellison Institute of Technology in Oxford.

I am especially interested in cases where small rules have surprisingly large effects.

  • Policy design and institutional incentives
  • Behavioural responses to rules and information
  • Applied empirical analysis for decision-making

Featured project

Do symbolic penalties work?

A $0.05 fine that is almost never enforced is associated with a ~20-point jump in turnout at age 18 — suggesting that laws can change behaviour even without credible enforcement.

This project uses Argentina’s compulsory voting threshold as a clean test of whether legal obligation can affect behaviour in the absence of deterrence. The evidence points to expressive compliance: the law operates as a normative signal rather than a credible threat.
