Symbolic penalties and civic participation

I study whether legal obligations can change behavior even when enforcement is limited. Using Argentina’s youth voting rules, I estimate the causal impact of becoming legally required to vote at age 18.

Behavioral public policy Compliance and institutions Youth participation Causal inference (RDD)

Executive takeaway

The shift from optional voting (ages 16–17) to compulsory voting (age 18+) produces a large increase in turnout, despite an abstention fine that is nominal and rarely enforced. The evidence is consistent with a norm-signaling channel: legal obligation itself appears to move behavior.

Why this is useful for policy and practice

This is a general result about how “light-touch” mandates can work when enforcement capacity is limited. It speaks to program design in settings where compliance is essential but monitoring is costly.

Examples include registration and reporting requirements, civic participation initiatives, and other rules where the main channel may be clarity and legitimacy rather than deterrence.

Approach (plain language)

I use a regression discontinuity design at the age-18 threshold. Individuals just below and just above 18 are comparable, but they face different legal voting status. The discontinuity in turnout at the cutoff identifies the causal effect of legal obligation under weak enforcement.
