Symbolic penalties and civic participation

This thesis asks whether laws can change behavior even when material enforcement is weak. Using Argentina’s voting rules for 16–20 year-olds, I estimate the causal impact of becoming legally required to vote at age 18.

Executive takeaway

The transition from optional voting (ages 16–17) to compulsory voting (age 18+) generates a large increase in turnout, despite a nominal abstention fine that is economically negligible and rarely enforced. The evidence is consistent with a norm-signaling channel: legal obligation itself appears to shift behavior.

What a policymaker should take from this:

Small, low-cost legal obligations can meaningfully shift participation when they operate as clear public signals. The design here separates the effect of obligation from substantial deterrence.

Policy relevance

  • Design of “light-touch” mandates: if enforcement is limited, clarity and salience of the rule matter.
  • Youth engagement: obligation can reduce participation gaps, especially where baseline turnout is low.
  • Beyond voting: results speak to compliance with low-enforcement rules in public programs (e.g., registration, reporting, civic requirements).

This framing is intentionally general so it reads well for consulting, policy, and applied roles.

Approach (in plain language)

I use a regression discontinuity design around the age-18 threshold. Individuals just below and just above 18 are similar in observable ways, but they face different legal voting status. The discontinuity in turnout at the cutoff identifies the causal effect of legal obligation under weak enforcement.

Data combine administrative election records across multiple national elections and complementary survey evidence on beliefs and motivations.
