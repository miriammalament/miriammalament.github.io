Do Symbolic Penalties Work?

Causal evidence from Argentina’s compulsory voting law: a sharp turnout jump at age 18 even when sanctions are tiny and rarely enforced.

Regression discontinuity (age-18 cutoff) Administrative elections data (2015–2023) Mechanisms: expressive compliance vs deterrence
Read the paper (PDF) Slides Replication

One-paragraph summary

Argentina’s abstention penalty is formally codified but economically negligible: a fixed 50-peso fine that has eroded to just a few cents in real value, with extremely limited enforcement. Exploiting the 2012 reform that made voting optional at 16–17 but compulsory from 18 onward, I estimate a sharp regression discontinuity at the age-18 threshold. Using administrative records from 15 national elections, turnout increases by about 20 percentage points at age 18. Survey evidence is more consistent with expressive compliance than deterrence: the law appears to act as a norm signal that activates civic duty rather than fear of punishment.

Why this matters

  • Identification: isolates legal obligation from meaningful enforcement using a sharp age rule.
  • Mechanism: provides evidence that laws can work as normative signals (expressive function of law).
  • Inclusion: suggests symbolic mandates can disproportionately mobilize under-represented youth.
