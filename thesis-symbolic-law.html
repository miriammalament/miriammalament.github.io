Policy evidence · Institutions and behavior

Symbolic penalties and civic participation

I study whether legal obligations can change behavior when enforcement is limited. Using Argentina’s youth voting rules, I estimate the causal impact of becoming legally required to vote at age 18.

Executive takeaway

The shift from optional voting (ages 16–17) to compulsory voting (age 18+) produces a large increase in turnout, despite an abstention fine that is nominal and rarely enforced. The evidence is consistent with a norm-signaling channel: legal obligation itself appears to move behavior.

Policy relevance

This speaks to the design of “light-touch” mandates in settings where monitoring is costly. When enforcement capacity is limited, the formal status of a rule can still matter if it is salient, credible, and socially legible.

Implications for practice

Where this generalizes beyond voting:

  • Registration, reporting, or compliance requirements where enforcement is thin.
  • Public programs that rely on broad participation and low administrative burden.
  • Institutional design questions where legitimacy and clarity may substitute for deterrence.

Approach (plain language)

I use a regression discontinuity design at the age-18 threshold. Individuals just below and just above 18 are comparable, but they face different legal voting status. The discontinuity in turnout at the cutoff identifies the causal effect of legal obligation under weak enforcement.
