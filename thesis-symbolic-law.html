Symbolic penalties and civic participation

Executive takeaway

The shift from optional voting (ages 16–17) to compulsory voting (age 18+) produces a large increase in turnout, despite an abstention fine that is nominal and rarely enforced. The evidence is consistent with a norm-signaling channel: legal obligation itself appears to move behavior.

Put differently: when enforcement is weak, the law can still matter if it is salient, legible, and treated as a real obligation.

Why this matters

Many policies rely on compliance where monitoring is costly. This study shows that the formal status of a rule can still matter if it is salient, credible, and socially legible, even when punishment is weak.

This is the broader applied point: symbolic enforcement is not the same as “no policy,” because the rule can shift norms and expectations.

Implications for practice

  • Light-touch mandates: contexts where enforcement is thin but rules are public and clear.
  • Administrative thresholds: eligibility or registration cutoffs that change perceived obligation.
  • Participation programs: settings where legitimacy and clarity can substitute for deterrence.

At a glance

Topic: symbolic enforcement and compliance
Setting: Argentina, youth voting rules
Method: sharp RDD at age 18

Optional detail: What is “expressive compliance”?

Behaviour can change not because people fear punishment, but because the law shifts expectations about what is normal or required. In that view, even symbolic penalties can generate meaningful participation responses.

Approach (plain language)

I use a regression discontinuity design at the age-18 threshold. Individuals just below and just above 18 are comparable, but they face different legal voting status. The turnout discontinuity at the cutoff identifies the causal effect of legal obligation under weak enforcement.
