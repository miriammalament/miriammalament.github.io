Research

My work sits at the intersection of institutions and behaviour. I am interested in settings where rules exist on paper but enforcement is weak, and where the central question is why behaviour changes anyway. Empirically, I work with administrative and survey microdata and prioritise transparent, reproducible design.

Featured research

Do symbolic penalties work?

A $0.05 fine that is almost never enforced still coincides with a ~20-point jump in turnout at age 18.

My master’s thesis studies Argentina’s compulsory voting threshold as a clean test of whether legal obligation can change behaviour even when deterrence is basically absent. The evidence is most consistent with expressive compliance: the law works as a normative signal rather than a credible threat.

What is “expressive compliance”?

Behaviour can change not because people fear punishment, but because the law signals a shared norm (what citizens are expected to do). In that view, even symbolic penalties can matter.

Macro stabilization · Expectations · 2023

Programas de Estabilización Anticipados

What changes when a stabilization program is announced in advance, rather than introduced as a surprise? I study how anticipation and credibility reshape the classic boom–bust patterns in money-based and exchange-rate-based stabilizations.

The core takeaway is about timing and beliefs: once policy is anticipated, the dynamics can flip relative to the standard “surprise” stabilization playbook.

PDF Record
What is the “anticipation” angle?

If households and firms adjust before the policy is implemented, the transition path matters as much as the policy itself. The paper uses that lens to compare MBS vs ERBS dynamics under advance announcements.

Fiscal institutions · Macro policy · Feb 2023

Hacia la construcción de un fondo fiscal anticíclico

A practical framework for designing an anti-cyclical fiscal fund when revenue is volatile and politics makes smoothing hard.

The contribution is deliberately second-best: it puts constraints on the table (market access, credibility, political economy) and builds a fund logic that is implementable, not idealized. (Co-authored with Mariano Fernández.)

PDF Record
What is the fund design question?

How large should the buffer be, what triggers saving vs spending, and how should rules respond to the cycle? The paper lays out the sizing logic and illustrates it with Argentina-style revenue dynamics.

Undergraduate thesis · Applied micro · Argentina · 2023

Retornos a la Educación e Inversión en Capital Humano: El Caso Argentino

What is the payoff to schooling in Argentina, and has it changed? I estimate returns to education using household survey microdata and track how the relationship evolves over time.

The emphasis is on comparability and interpretability: a standard Mincer setup, carefully aligned across waves, plus results by education level.

PDF Record
What is the empirical core?

Standard earnings regressions (Mincer) using EPH microdata, with a focus on tracking estimates consistently across time and making the trend easy to read.

Other work

Selected collaborative research

I also assisted some cool research, often focusing on data work and reproducible pipelines. Below are a few papers I have supported.

Labour · Field experiments · NBER WP 33903

Pushing the Envelope: The Effects of Salary Negotiations

What happens when more workers negotiate? Using large-scale field experiments, the paper studies how negotiation affects wages, and whether the returns differ across workers and workplaces.

The design links negotiation behaviour to pay outcomes and speaks directly to information frictions and inequality in wage setting.

NBER page PDF
What I would take away in one line

Negotiation is not just a personality trait; it responds to the environment—and changing that environment can move pay.

Political economy · Beliefs · NBER WP 32712

Billionaire Superstar: Public Image and Demand for Taxation

Why do people support (or oppose) taxing the very rich? The paper uses a survey experiment to test how narratives about billionaires shift beliefs, perceived fairness, and preferences for redistribution.

The central idea is that “who billionaires are” in the public imagination—innovators, rent-seekers, philanthropists—changes what people think the tax system should do.

NBER page PDF
What makes it interesting

Preferences over taxation are not only about self-interest; they are also about beliefs—and beliefs can be moved by framing.

Macro expectations · Consumer finance · NBER WP 28281

Macroeconomic Expectations and Credit Card Spending

Do expectations change behaviour? The paper provides information that shifts inflation and exchange rate expectations and then tracks how consumers adjust spending using transaction-level credit card data.

It is a clean link between beliefs and real choices: when expectations move, spending patterns move in measurable ways—especially for goods where timing and intertemporal substitution matter.

NBER page PDF
One-line intuition

If you change what people think will happen to prices, you can change what they buy today.
