The Five-Cent Law: How a Symbolic Fine Gets 20% More People to Vote

Blog entry · Date: replace when publishing · 6–8 minute read

Why do we follow the rules? Is it fear of getting caught, the sting of a penalty, or something else entirely? A remarkable case from Argentina challenges the usual deterrence story. Sometimes the most powerful laws are the ones with no teeth.

In Argentina, voting has been compulsory for over a century. It is more than a rule on paper. Through a turbulent history that included periods of military rule, voting later became a constitutional right, and it carries a strong symbolic meaning of democratic belonging.

Here is the puzzle. The penalty for not voting is a 50-peso fine. Once worth about $2.50, years of inflation have pushed its real value down to around five U.S. cents, and it is rarely collected. From a purely rational perspective, this should have little effect. And yet, it does. How can a law with no real punishment still change who shows up to the polls?

A legal switch flips, and nearly 20% more people show up

The rules change abruptly at age 18. For 16- and 17-year-olds, voting is optional. The moment a citizen turns 18, voting becomes a legal obligation. That single legal switch is followed by a sharp, discontinuous change in behaviour.

As soon as Argentinians become legally required to vote, turnout jumps by about 19.7 percentage points. This is not a gradual shift. It happens right at the age-18 threshold and is stable across electoral cycles. Nothing else about these young people changes overnight. The key change is legal status: a voluntary act becomes a civic duty.
