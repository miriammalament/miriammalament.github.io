The Five-Cent Law: How a Symbolic Fine Gets 20% More People to Vote

Blog entry · Date: replace when publishing · 6–8 minute read

Why do we follow the rules? Is it fear of getting caught, the sting of a penalty, or something else entirely? A remarkable case from Argentina challenges the usual deterrence story. Sometimes the most powerful laws are the ones with no teeth.

In Argentina, voting has been compulsory for over a century. It is more than a rule on paper. Through a turbulent history that included periods of military rule, voting later became a constitutional right, and it carries a strong symbolic meaning of democratic belonging.

Here is the puzzle. The penalty for not voting is a 50-peso fine. Once worth about $2.50, years of inflation have pushed its real value down to around five U.S. cents, and it is rarely collected. From a purely rational perspective, this should have little effect. And yet, it does. How can a law with no real punishment still change who shows up to the polls?

A legal switch flips, and nearly 20% more people show up

The rules change abruptly at age 18. For 16- and 17-year-olds, voting is optional. The moment a citizen turns 18, voting becomes a legal obligation. That single legal switch is followed by a sharp, discontinuous change in behaviour.

As soon as Argentinians become legally required to vote, turnout jumps by about 19.7 percentage points. This is not a gradual shift. It happens right at the age-18 threshold and is stable across electoral cycles. Nothing else about these young people changes overnight. The key change is legal status: a voluntary act becomes a civic duty.

It is not the threat, it is the signal

If it is not fear of a five-cent fine, what is driving the behaviour? The evidence points to a mechanism often described as expressive compliance. People comply not because they fear punishment, but because the law signals a social expectation and activates civic duty.

  • Deterrence: If the fine were the driver, beliefs about enforcement should matter. Yet even people confident that sanctions are not enforced still report high voting intention. Some precautionary voting may exist at the margin, but it does not explain the magnitude.
  • Civic maturation: Turning 18 does not instantly change political interest or democratic attitudes. These attitudes move smoothly with age, unlike turnout, which jumps sharply at the legal threshold.
The law’s effectiveness may lie not in the threat it poses, but in the meaning it conveys.

A symbolic law can be a great equalizer

One of the most striking results is who responds most. The turnout increase is especially pronounced among lower-income and lower-education youth. In other words, a symbolic mandate can disproportionately mobilize groups that are often underrepresented in politics.

In that sense, the legal obligation can function like a civic rite of passage. By codifying voting as a duty, the state clarifies what is expected of citizens, and the signal appears to close participation gaps without relying on coercion.

The quiet power of shared expectations

Argentina’s “five-cent law” illustrates a broader point about behaviour: rules can work through norms, not only through penalties. The sharp discontinuity at age 18 highlights how a legal mandate can act as a normative signal, especially for youth who are otherwise hardest to mobilize.

It raises a question that applies far beyond elections. What if the best way to encourage civic behaviour is not with bigger sticks, but with clearer signals about what a community expects and values?
