I build empirical evidence for practical policy and strategy questions: when rules change, who responds, by how much, and why. My work uses administrative and survey microdata, clean identification, and clear takeaways.

Featured - Master's thesis

Do Symbolic Penalties Work? Evidence from Argentina’s Compulsory Voting Law

A $0.05 fine that is almost never enforced still lines up with a ~20pp jump in turnout at age 18.

The project tests whether legal obligation can change behavior when deterrence is basically absent. Using the age-18 threshold, patterns are most consistent with the law acting as a social signal rather than a credible threat.

  • ~20pp turnout jump
  • 15 national elections (2015–2023)
  • $0.05 real fine (approx.)

Macroeconomics · Expectations · 2023

Announced Stabilization Programs: Expectations and Macro Dynamics

Problem: Stabilization plans are often evaluated as “surprises,” but many are announced early.

What I did: Studied how anticipation and credibility shift the transition path in money-based and exchange-rate-based stabilizations.

Why it matters: Timing changes the mechanism. Expectations move first, and the boom–bust pattern can flip.

Fiscal Policy · Institutions · 2023

Designing a Countercyclical Fiscal Fund

Problem: Volatile revenues and political constraints make smoothing difficult in practice.

What I did: Built an implementable fund framework that makes constraints explicit (market access, credibility, political economy).

Why it matters: It turns “save in booms, spend in busts” into rules that can survive real-world incentives.

Labor · Human Capital · Argentina · 2023

Returns to Education and Human Capital Investment: Evidence from Argentina

Problem: How large is the payoff to schooling, and how stable is it over time?

What I did: Estimated returns to education using household survey microdata, aligned consistently across waves.

Why it matters: Clear, comparable estimates help interpret wage inequality and the incentives to invest in education.
